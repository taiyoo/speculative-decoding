\begin{abstract}
Speculative decoding accelerates large language model inference by using a smaller draft model to propose token blocks that are verified by a target model. We present a controlled empirical study of speculative decoding, with a reproducibility-focused protocol across reasoning, knowledge, and summarization tasks. Using a fixed target model and two same-family draft models, we evaluate draft length, temperature, and task type on GSM8K, MMLU subsets, and CNN/DailyMail. We report latency, throughput, time-to-first-token, token acceptance dynamics, and task quality, and analyze the trade-off frontier between speed and output fidelity. Our results show that speculative decoding yields a consistent speedup of [X]--[Y]$\times$ in deterministic decoding, with quality changes bounded within [Z] points under the best configuration. We further find that acceptance rate is the dominant mediator of end-to-end gains, and that its sensitivity to temperature and task entropy explains most cross-task variance in speedup. Finally, we provide practical operating guidelines for selecting draft model size and draft length under explicit quality constraints, along with a fully reproducible protocol.
\end{abstract}
