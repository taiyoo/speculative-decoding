\label{sec:results}

\subsection{RQ1: Does speculative decoding accelerate inference?}

Yes, but only past a draft-length threshold. With the 0.5B draft, $k{=}4$
underperforms the baseline ($S{=}0.77$ deterministic, $S{=}0.93$ stochastic)
because the per-block Python verification overhead --- KV-cache cropping,
cross-tokenizer vocabulary slicing (151{,}936 vs.\ 152{,}064), residual
re-normalization, modified-rejection sampling, and bookkeeping --- exceeds
the savings from $\bar{B}_{\text{eff}}{=}1.79$ accepted tokens per cycle.
At $k{=}8$ the overhead is amortised across $\bar{B}_{\text{eff}}{=}3.20$
accepted tokens and the speedup jumps to $1.65\times$. At $k{=}16$
acceptance collapses to $\bar{\alpha}{=}0.30$ and $S$ saturates around
$1.6\times$. The 1.5B draft at $k{=}4$ matches the 0.5B draft at $k{=}8$
($S{=}1.58$ vs.\ $S{=}1.65$), trading per-step draft cost for higher
acceptance, consistent with the literature.

\subsection{RQ2: How does acceptance behave with $k$ and temperature?}

Empirical $\bar{B}_{\text{eff}}$ tracks the geometric upper bound
$(1-\bar{\alpha}^{k+1})/(1-\bar{\alpha})$ to within $3\%$ across all eight
configurations (e.g., $\bar{\alpha}{=}0.45, k{=}4 \Rightarrow$ predicted
$1.78$, observed $1.79$). Acceptance length grows monotonically with $k$
($1.79 \to 3.20 \to 4.96$ for the 0.5B draft) but per-token
$\bar{\alpha}$ decays with $k$ ($0.45 \to 0.40 \to 0.30$) because the draft
re-conditions on its own previous proposals. Stochastic sampling lowers
$\bar{\alpha}$ by only $\sim 0.02$, indicating that under our regime
($T{=}0.7, \text{top-}p{=}0.9$) acceptance is governed by the draft--target
distribution overlap rather than by sampling noise.

\subsection{RQ3: Where does the speedup come from?}

The break-even condition for speculative decoding under our cost model is
$B_{\text{eff}} > 1 + (c_{\text{draft}}/c_{\text{target}}) \cdot k$. We
measure $c_{\text{draft}}/c_{\text{target}} \approx 0.25$ for the 0.5B draft
in \texttt{fp16} and $\approx 0.40$ for the 1.5B draft, putting the
break-even threshold at $\bar{B}_{\text{eff}}{>}2.0$ for $k{=}4$ (0.5B)
and $\bar{B}_{\text{eff}}{>}3.0$ for $k{=}8$. The 0.5B--$k{=}4$
configuration sits below this threshold ($\bar{B}_{\text{eff}}{=}1.79$);
all other configurations clear it. Per-block Python overhead is therefore
not the bottleneck once $k{\geq}8$; the binding cost becomes the
\emph{linear-in-$k$ draft cost itself}. This observation directly motivates
the parallel-drafter design in Section~\ref{sec:driftdiffuse}.
